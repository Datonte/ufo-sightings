CASE STUDY: THE NIMITZ INCIDENT

November 14, 2004. USS Nimitz Carrier Strike Group, Pacific Ocean, approximately 100 miles southwest of San Diego. Commander David Fravor and Lt. Cmdr. Jim Slaight intercept an unidentified aerial vehicle — white, oblong, Tic-Tac shaped, approximately 40 feet in length — exhibiting flight characteristics inconsistent with any known propulsion system. The encounter lasted approximately 5 minutes before the object accelerated beyond visual range.

warning Intel Notes

RESTRICTED

USS Princeton (CG-70) tracked anomalous objects on SPY-1B radar for approximately two weeks prior to first visual intercept. Objects appeared at REDACTED feet, then descended to approximately 50 feet above sea level in under 0.78 seconds — equivalent to REDACTED times the speed of sound, producing no sonic boom.

Object described by Cdr. Fravor as white, oblong, Tic-Tac shaped — approximately 40 feet in length. No visible wings, rotors, exhaust plumes, or control surfaces. Bilateral symmetry observed. A churning disturbance of white water, roughly the size of a REDACTED, was observed on the sea surface directly below the stationary object.

After Fravor's attempted intercept maneuver, the object mirrored his flight path, then accelerated to beyond visual range instantaneously. USS Princeton re-acquired the object at Fravor's pre-briefed Combat Air Patrol point — a REDACTED location — in under 60 seconds, covering approximately 60 miles. No known propulsion system can achieve this.

FLIR1 footage captured by WSO Lt. Chad Underwood aboard a second F/A-18F during a subsequent intercept. Original footage classified at time of recording. A copy was leaked in 2007. The Department of Defense officially declassified and authenticated the footage on April 27, 2020.

STATUS: NO OFFICIAL EXPLANATION PROVIDED BY THE US GOVERNMENT AS OF 2024.

schedule Event Timeline

NOV 1–14, 2004

ANOMALOUS RADAR CONTACTS

USS Princeton SPY-1B radar tracks multiple unidentified objects operating in restricted airspace off San Diego coast. Objects appear at high altitude, emit no transponder signal, produce no heat signature, and perform maneuvers beyond known aircraft capability.

NOV 14, 2004 — 14:02 LOCAL

FIRST VISUAL INTERCEPT

Cdr. David Fravor and Lt. Cmdr. Alex Dietrich launched to intercept. Fravor observes Tic-Tac object and attempts to cut it off in a spiral descent. Object mirrors his maneuver exactly, then disappears in under a second.

NOV 14, 2004 — 14:15 LOCAL

SECOND INTERCEPT / FLIR FOOTAGE CAPTURED

Second flight of F/A-18s dispatched. WSO Lt. Chad Underwood's FLIR system locks onto the object. Footage captured shows object rotating in flight. Object departs at extreme speed before visual intercept can be made.

2009–2017

CLASSIFIED / AATIP PROGRAM

FLIR1 footage circulates internally among cleared personnel. Luis Elizondo's AATIP (Advanced Aerospace Threat Identification Program) funds investigation under the Defense Intelligence Agency. Pilots told to keep the encounter quiet.

DEC 16, 2017

NEW YORK TIMES DISCLOSURE

New York Times publishes 'Glowing Auras and Black Money' exposing the AATIP program. FLIR1, Gimbal, and GoFast videos published simultaneously. Luis Elizondo resigns from Pentagon in protest over handling of UAP intelligence.

APR 27, 2020

PENTAGON OFFICIAL RELEASE

Department of Defense formally declassifies and releases all three videos. Official statement: 'The aerial phenomena observed in the videos remain characterized as unidentified.' No further explanation provided.
